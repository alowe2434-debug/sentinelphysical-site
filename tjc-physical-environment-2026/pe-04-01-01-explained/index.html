PE.04.01.01 Explained:
The 2026 ITM Standard for
Fire & Smoke Dampers

Effective January 1, 2026
Replaces EC.02.05.07 & LS.02.01.10
NFPA 80 · NFPA 105

PE.04.01.01 is the 2026 TJC standard governing Inspection, Testing, and Maintenance (ITM) for fire and smoke dampers in accredited healthcare facilities. It replaced EC.02.05.07 and consolidated language from LS.02.01.10, becoming the single governing standard for fire and smoke damper documentation effective January 1, 2026.

If your Life Safety Logbook, vendor inspection reports, or Maintenance Management Program still reference EC.02.05.07, your documentation does not align with the current survey framework — even if the physical inspections were completed correctly.

Why PE.04.01.01 Matters Now

The Joint Commission's 2026 Physical Environment chapter consolidation restructured nearly 40 standards. PE.04.01.01 sits at the center of this restructuring because fire and smoke damper compliance is one of the most frequently cited Physical Environment deficiencies in TJC surveys nationwide.

Under the old framework, damper-related findings were distributed across EC.02.05.07 (the ITM standard), LS.02.01.10 (fire features), and several related utility system standards. Under PE.04.01.01, all of this is consolidated into a single standard with clearer documentation requirements and a more direct path to a Requirement for Improvement (RFI) if documentation gaps exist.

Survey Risk

A surveyor reviewing your facility in 2026 will assess Physical Environment documentation against PE.04.01.01, not EC.02.05.07. Documentation that cannot be mapped to PE.04.01.01 elements creates an immediate finding, regardless of whether the underlying inspections were performed.

What PE.04.01.01 Actually Requires

PE.04.01.01 has several distinct elements, each of which requires specific documentation to be survey-ready. Here is what each element covers and what constitutes a compliance gap.

PE.04
.01.01
(a)
Complete Asset Inventory
Every fire and smoke damper in the facility must be inventoried and documented. This includes dampers in mechanical rooms, ceiling plenums, and non-standard locations that may have been missed by previous vendors.
● High survey risk if incomplete
PE.04
.01.01
(b)
ITM per NFPA 80 / NFPA 105
Inspections must follow NFPA 80 (fire dampers, typically every 4 years) and NFPA 105 (smoke dampers) intervals. Each inspection must produce timestamped open/closed actuation records for every damper.
● High survey risk if overdue or undocumented
PE.04
.01.01
(c)
Contractor Credentials on File
All technicians performing damper inspections must hold CFSDI-1 or equivalent certification. The certificate must be documented in the Life Safety Logbook — not just held by the contractor.
● Medium risk — often overlooked
PE.04
.01.01
(d)
MMP Documentation
The Maintenance Management Program must reference PE.04.01.01 — not legacy EC/LS chapter nomenclature. This is an administrative requirement but a common finding in 2026 surveys.
● Medium risk — administrative gap
PE.04
.01.01
(e)
Deficiency Tracking & Remediation
Any damper that fails actuation testing must be documented as a deficiency and tracked through remediation. Open deficiencies without a remediation timeline are a Red-level finding.
● High survey risk if untracked

EC.02.05.07 vs. PE.04.01.01: What Changed

The shift from EC.02.05.07 to PE.04.01.01 is not purely cosmetic. The underlying NFPA 80 and NFPA 105 requirements did not change — but the documentation standard, the crosswalk between standards, and the surveyor's review framework all changed on January 1, 2026.

Pre-2026: EC.02.05.07 Framework

Damper ITM referenced across EC.02.05.07, LS.02.01.10, and related standards. Documentation organized by chapter. Survey findings distributed across EC and LS chapter citations. Checklist-based assessment approach.

2026: PE.04.01.01 Framework

All damper ITM consolidated into PE.04.01.01. Single standard reference for all documentation. Survey findings cited under the PE chapter. Surveyors review against the new Physical Environment framework, expecting documentation mapped to PE elements rather than legacy EC and LS chapter references.

The practical impact: a facility whose documentation was fully current under the 2025 framework has a documentation gap in 2026 — not because anything changed about their physical compliance program, but because the standard their documents reference no longer matches what surveyors are reviewing against.

NFPA 80 Inspection Intervals Under PE.04.01.01

PE.04.01.01 explicitly references NFPA 80 and NFPA 105 for inspection frequency requirements. The most common interval questions:

Damper Type Standard Base Interval Exception
Fire dampers (all) NFPA 80 Every 4 years 1 year if fusible links in high-dust/grease environment
Smoke dampers NFPA 105 Every 4 years More frequent if smoke control system requires it
Combination fire/smoke NFPA 80 + 105 Every 4 years Must meet requirements of both standards
Post-repair reinspection PE.04.01.01 (e) Before return to service No grace period — must be documented before closing deficiency

The 4-year interval gap

Facilities that completed a full damper inspection in 2021 or 2022 are either approaching or already past their NFPA 80 reinspection date. Under PE.04.01.01, an overdue inspection is a deficiency whether or not the previous inspection found any issues. Do not assume a clean prior inspection carries forward indefinitely.

How to Document PE.04.01.01 Compliance

Survey readiness under PE.04.01.01 requires more than completed inspection reports. The documentation package a surveyor expects to see includes:

  • A complete damper asset inventory with unique IDs for each damper
  • Inspection reports citing PE.04.01.01 (not EC.02.05.07) with per-damper actuation results
  • Timestamped photographic evidence for each damper inspected
  • CFSDI-1 or equivalent contractor certification on file
  • MMP documentation referencing the current PE chapter framework
  • Open deficiency log with remediation timeline for any failed dampers
  • Program summary documenting overall ITM status aligned to PE.04.01.01 elements
Key Point

The most common PE.04.01.01 documentation gap is not missing inspections — it is inspection records that reference the wrong standard. An inspection report citing EC.02.05.07 in 2026 is a documentation gap, even if the inspection was performed correctly and on schedule. The crosswalk between old and new standard language must be explicit in your Logbook.
